# Colorado 2025 Ballot Measures — Healthy School Meals for All
## Analysis Notes & Sources

---

## Overview

Colorado's **Healthy School Meals for All** program launched in the 2023–24 school year, making Colorado one of the first states to provide universal free school meals statewide. The 2025 ballot measures were designed to secure and expand the program's ongoing funding.

---

## The Ballot Measures

### Proposition LL — Maintain Healthy School Meals Funding
**Type:** TABOR retention (no new taxes)

Colorado's Taxpayer's Bill of Rights (TABOR) requires voter approval to retain tax revenues above a legislatively set cap. Proposition LL asked voters to allow the state to keep $12.4 million already collected — money that would otherwise be refunded to taxpayers — and redirect it to the Healthy School Meals program.

- **Fiscal impact:** State retains $12.4M already in the program's account. No new taxes.
- **Vote meaning:** YES = keep funds in the program. NO = return funds to taxpayers (~$8/taxpayer).
- **Result:** Passed.

**Sources:**
- Colorado Legislative Council Staff, *Fiscal Note: Proposition LL* (2025)
- Colorado Secretary of State, *2025 State Ballot Information Booklet* ("Blue Book")

---

### Proposition MM — Fund Free School Meals for All Colorado Students
**Type:** Income tax deduction limit change

Proposition MM lowered the cap on itemized income tax deductions for high-income households:
- Single filers earning $300K+: deduction cap reduced from $12,000 → $1,000
- Joint filers earning $300K+: deduction cap reduced from $16,000 → $2,000

This raises approximately **$95 million per year**, directed to:
1. Free K–12 school meals for all students
2. Higher wages for school food service workers
3. Local food purchasing support
4. Partial SNAP implementation costs

- **Affected households:** ~5.5% of Colorado filers (those earning $300K+/year)
- **Average fiscal impact on affected households:** +$486/year in additional taxes
- **Result:** Passed.

**Sources:**
- Colorado Legislative Council Staff, *Fiscal Note: Proposition MM* (2025)
- Colorado Secretary of State, *2025 State Ballot Information Booklet* ("Blue Book")
- Colorado Fiscal Institute, *Analysis of Proposition MM* (2025)

## What Peer-Reviewed Research Shows on Student Outcomes

Five states enacted statewide universal free school meals between 2022 and 2023 (California, Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Vermont). Because these programs are recent, rigorous causal studies isolating their specific effects on attendance, test scores, and mental health have not yet been published. The evidence base therefore draws from two sources:

1. **State trend data** — descriptive statistics from those states' first 1–2 years
2. **Peer-reviewed research** — causal studies on earlier programs (Community Eligibility Provision, universal free breakfast, etc.), which provide the best available estimates of what universal meals do to student outcomes

---

### Attendance / Chronic Absenteeism

**Research finding (strongest evidence):**
A study of 132,353 NYC kindergarteners in schools with universal free meals found that chronic absenteeism dropped **5.4 percentage points** among meal recipients versus non-recipients, equivalent to **1.8 additional school days per year**. Attendance effects diminished but persisted into 2nd grade (2.2 pp gap).

> Schwartz & Trajkovski, *Exposure to Free School Meals in Kindergarten Has Lasting Positive Effects on Students' Attendance*, Syracuse University Lerner Center (2023)

A study of free breakfast in the classroom versus cafeteria found students gained **1.12 additional school days per year** when breakfast was universally free and served in the classroom.

> Walker et al. (2021), cited in *Breakfast and School-Related Outcomes in Children and Adolescents in the US*, PMC (2022)

A 2024 systematic review of 6 studies covering 11,000+ U.S. schools found attendance rates "did not change or were modestly improved" — with one study finding a significant reduction in "low attendance" events in year 2. The review rated certainty of evidence as **low**, reflecting the difficulty of isolating the meals effect from other school interventions.

> Vercammen et al., *Universal Free School Meals and School and Student Outcomes: A Systematic Review*, JAMA Network Open (2024)

**State trend data:**

| State | Attendance Trend | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Massachusetts | Chronic absenteeism fell **4.9 pp** in year 2 (2023→2024) — steepest single-year decline in state history | Governor and Secretary of Education cited universal meals as a contributing factor alongside attendance campaigns and mental health supports |
| Minnesota | Chronic absenteeism fell **1 pp** in first year (25.5% → 24.5%) | MDE first-year summary; concurrent school suspension policy changes make isolation difficult |
| California | Chronic absenteeism fell from **30%** (2022) to **20.4%** (2024) over two years | CDE School Dashboard; meals cited among several concurrent interventions (community schools, expanded learning, mental health) |
| Maine | No published before/after attendance data attributing changes to the program | — |
| Vermont | No published before/after attendance data attributing changes to the program | — |

**Sources:**
- Massachusetts DESE; Healey-Driscoll Administration press release, "Massachusetts Schools with Biggest Drop in Chronic Absenteeism" (2024)
- Minnesota Department of Education, *Free School Meals: First Year Preliminary Summary* (2024)
- California Department of Education School Dashboard (2022–24); PACE, *Unpacking California's Chronic Absence Crisis Through 2023-24*
- FRAC interview with MA Secretary of Education Patrick Tutwiler (2024)

### Academic Performance (Test Scores)

**Research finding (strongest evidence):**
Three peer-reviewed studies using quasi-experimental or difference-in-differences designs find that universal or free-for-all breakfast improves math scores by approximately **0.06–0.10 standard deviations**, with smaller and less consistent effects on reading:

| Study | Design | Math Effect | Reading Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Imberman & Kugler (2014) | Schools shifting breakfast from cafeteria to classroom, quasi-experimental | +0.10 SD | +0.10 SD |
| Gordanier et al. (2020) | South Carolina CEP adoption, difference-in-differences | +0.06 SD (elementary) | Not significant |
| Frisvold (2015) | Universal free breakfast | +0.08 SD | +0.05 SD |

A 2022 study using national CEP variation found test score improvements concentrated in **districts where universal access was a larger expansion** (i.e., districts with previously low free-meal participation rates). No significant effect in districts already at near-universal eligibility.

> Ruffini (2022), *Universal Access to Free School Meals and Student Achievement: Evidence from the CEP*, Journal of Human Resources, Vol. 57(3)

The 2024 JAMA Network Open systematic review found **no studies** specifically examining standardized test score effects of the 2022-23 state universal meal programs.

**State trend data:**

| State | Academic Trend | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Massachusetts | Science scores improved (grades 5 & 10); MCAS ELA declined 2–6 pp in grades 3–5 | Trends attributed to pandemic recovery generally; no analysis isolating meals effect |
| Minnesota | Math and reading scores flat (43% and 48% meeting grade level) | MDE test data (2024–25); program too new for causal analysis |
| California | Multi-year academic evaluation ongoing (UC ANR / Nutrition Policy Institute) | No published test score outcome data yet; anticipated through SY 2025-26 |
| Maine | No published test score outcome data | — |
| Vermont | No published test score outcome data | — |

**Sources:**
- NBER Working Paper 17720 (Imberman & Kugler, 2014)
- *Economics of Education Review*, Gordanier et al. (2020)
- *Journal of Human Resources*, Ruffini (2022)
- Massachusetts DESE MCAS reports (2023, 2024)
- Minnesota Reformer, "Minnesota students' academic performance stagnates after pandemic declines" (August 2025)
- UC ANR Nutrition Policy Institute, *Evaluation of Universal School Meals in California*, evaluation brief (2024–25)

### Student Mental Health and Wellbeing

This is the **largest evidence gap**. No peer-reviewed study has directly measured clinical mental health outcomes (anxiety, depression, or validated wellbeing instruments) specifically for the 2022-23 state universal meal programs. What exists:

**Indirect causal chain (well-established):**
Food insecurity is strongly associated with anxiety, depression, and behavioral problems in children. Universal meals reduce household food insecurity — confirmed by a 2025 study finding states with universal meal policies had significantly lower household food insecurity rates.

> AJPM (2025), *Statewide Universal School Meal Policies and Food Insecurity in Households With Children*

**Perceptual / observational evidence:**
- In Vermont's CEP-era program (pre-2022), **83% of school staff** agreed that free meals meant students were "more ready to learn."
  > Cited in *Universal School Meals and Associations with Student Participation, Attendance, Academic Performance, Diet Quality, Food Security, and BMI: A Systematic Review*, Nutrients (2021, PMC8000006)
- A UK quasi-experimental study of a universal free meals pilot found **no significant quantitative effects** on hunger or food insecurity, but qualitative interviews with students, families, and staff reported reduced stress, reduced hunger, and perceived improvements in school performance.
  > PubMed PMID 36982124 (2023)

**Early behavioral proxy evidence:**
- Schools with universally free breakfast in the classroom showed lower suspension rates compared to fee-based cafeteria service — suspensions used as a behavioral proxy for wellbeing.
  > Walker et al. (2021)
- FRAC references reduced nurse visit frequency in schools with universal free breakfast, attributed to a 2001–02 evaluation; this finding has not been replicated in recent literature.

**Summary:** The mental health case for universal meals rests primarily on the established link between food insecurity and child mental health outcomes, not yet on direct measurement of mental health effects from the 2022–23 state programs. This is an area where more research is needed and expected as these programs mature.

## Analysis Steps

1. **Identified the ballot measures** — Reviewed the Colorado Secretary of State's 2025 Blue Book (23 pages) for official measure text, fiscal notes, vote language, and all four pass/fail interaction scenarios for Prop LL × Prop MM.

2. **Framed the policy context** — Distinguished Prop LL (TABOR retention, no new taxes) from Prop MM (tax policy change) to make the voter choice clear and accurate.

3. **Researched peer-reviewed literature on student outcomes** — Identified key causal studies on universal free meals and attendance (Schwartz/Trajkovski 2023; Walker et al. 2021; JAMA systematic review 2024), test scores (Imberman/Kugler 2014; Gordanier et al. 2020; Ruffini 2022; Frisvold 2015), and mental health (AJPM 2025; UK quasi-experiment 2023).

4. **Gathered state-level trend data** — Pulled published state reports from MDE (Minnesota), DESE (Massachusetts), CDE (California), and FRAC's five-state report (2024). Note that Maine and Vermont have not published comparable before/after outcome data.

5. **Distinguished causal evidence from trend data** — The peer-reviewed literature provides causal estimates; the state trend data is descriptive and multi-causal. Kept these clearly separated throughout.

6. **Updated the UI** — Revised state cards to show attendance and academic trends rather than participation and stigma figures. Updated explanatory text to frame the research evidence accurately.

## Notes & Caveats

- **Causality is limited for the 2022–23 programs.** All five states implemented universal meals during or immediately after the pandemic recovery period. Disentangling meal effects from general COVID recovery, other concurrent policies, and post-pandemic normalization is not possible with currently available data.
- **The strongest causal evidence comes from older programs** (CEP studies, 2013–2020 data; universal breakfast studies). Whether effects are the same for statewide universality is an open empirical question.
- **Mental health is the largest evidence gap.** No peer-reviewed study with validated clinical instruments has measured anxiety, depression, or wellbeing outcomes specifically from these programs.
- **Massachusetts has the strongest state-specific signal** — a 4.9 pp chronic absenteeism decline in year 2 — but even that is attributed to multiple concurrent interventions by state officials.
- **Colorado's program had already been operating for one school year** (2023–24) before these ballot measures, so the 2025 vote was primarily about sustaining and expanding funding — not initiating the program.
- The $12.4M retained under Prop LL and the $95M/year raised under Prop MM are fiscal figures from the Colorado Legislative Council Staff's official fiscal notes.
